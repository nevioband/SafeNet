Man-in-the-Middle

What is a Man-in-the-Middle Attack?

In a Man-in-the-Middle (MITM) attack, an attacker secretly inserts themselves between two communicating parties â€“ for example between you and your bank's website. They can intercept, read, and even modify the communication without either side noticing. The name comes from the fact that the attacker literally sits "in the middle."

How does it work?

There are several techniques attackers use to carry out a MITM attack:

ARP Spoofing: On a local network (e.g. your home Wi-Fi), the attacker sends fake ARP packets. This tricks your computer and the router into thinking the attacker is the other device â€“ all traffic is routed through them.

Evil Twin Wi-Fi: The attacker creates a fake Wi-Fi hotspot with a familiar name (e.g. "Starbucks_Free_WiFi"). You connect, and all your data flows through the attacker's device.

SSL Stripping: If you connect over HTTP instead of HTTPS, the attacker can remove the encryption layer. You won't see a padlock in the browser and often won't notice anything is wrong.

DNS Poisoning: The attacker manipulates DNS responses so your browser loads a fake website instead of the real one â€“ even though you typed the correct address.

Risks and Consequences

A successful MITM attack can have serious consequences: The attacker can capture login credentials, passwords, and session tokens, steal banking details and credit card information, decrypt and read encrypted communications, inject malicious content into web pages (e.g. fake forms or malware), and use your identity for further attacks.

How to protect yourself

1. Always use HTTPS: Look for the padlock icon in your browser. Only visit websites with an HTTPS connection, especially for banking or logins. Browser extensions like HTTPS Everywhere can help.

2. Avoid public Wi-Fi for sensitive activities: Wi-Fi in cafÃ©s, hotels, or airports is insecure. Avoid logging into banks or email accounts on these networks.

3. Use a VPN: A Virtual Private Network encrypts all your traffic before it leaves your device. Even in a compromised network, attackers cannot read your data.

4. Verify SSL certificates: Click the padlock icon in your browser and check whether the certificate matches the website. A mismatched certificate may indicate a MITM attack.

5. Enable 2FA: Even if attackers intercept your password, two-factor authentication protects your account from unauthorised access.
