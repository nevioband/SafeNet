Quishing

What is Quishing?

Quishing combines "QR code" and "phishing" and refers to an attack method where cybercriminals use malicious QR codes to redirect victims to fake websites. Since QR codes are now everywhere â€“ on menus, posters, tickets, and in emails â€“ this method is becoming increasingly popular. Unlike classic phishing links, QR codes cannot be read by the human eye, making them much harder to detect.

How does it work?

Physical QR codes: Attackers paste fake QR code stickers over real ones â€“ for example on parking meters, restaurant tables, or advertising posters. Anyone who scans the code ends up on a phishing site instead of the real service.

QR codes in emails: Instead of a clickable link, the phishing email only contains a QR code image. Many email security filters do not scan images and let the message through. The victim scans the code with their smartphone and lands on a fake login page.

Fake documents: PDFs, invoices, or official letters contain QR codes that supposedly lead to payment or verification pages â€“ in reality they steal credentials or initiate a payment to the attacker.

Social media and messaging: QR codes are shared in direct messages or social media posts promising discounts, competitions, or exclusive content.

Risks and Consequences

Since smartphones are often less protected than desktop computers when scanning QR codes, the following risks can arise: theft of login credentials and passwords on fake websites, unwanted payments through manipulated payment pages, malware installation via automatic app downloads, takeover of social media or email accounts, and access to further accounts through stolen session tokens.

How to protect yourself

1. Check the URL before opening: Most QR scanners show the destination URL before opening it. Read the URL carefully: Is the domain correct? Does it match the expected service?

2. Be critical of physical QR codes: Is there a sticker placed over an already printed code? Does the code look improvised? If so, don't scan it.

3. Never log in after scanning a QR code without verification: If a page asks for your credentials after a QR code scan, be extra careful and double-check the URL.

4. Use a secure QR scanner app: Some apps have built-in phishing detection and warn about suspicious URLs before opening them.

5. When in doubt, type it manually: If you want to visit an official site (e.g. your bank), enter the URL directly in your browser rather than scanning a QR code.

Real-World Examples

Parking meter: You want to pay at a parking meter using a QR code. An attacker has placed a fake QR sticker over the real one. You are taken to a convincing payment page and enter your credit card details.

Restaurant menu: The restaurant uses QR codes for their digital menu. An attacker sticks their own code on top. You are redirected to a phishing site asking for a "table reservation login."

Phishing email: You receive an email supposedly from your bank asking you to scan a QR code to verify your account. The code leads to a perfectly replicated bank website â€“ you enter your credentials and the attacker has them immediately.
